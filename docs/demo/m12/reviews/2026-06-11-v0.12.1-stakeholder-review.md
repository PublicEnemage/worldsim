## Session Narrative

### Section 1 — The Problem

**Lucas** engages from the opening. The framing of the capability asymmetry — IMF team with proprietary models versus ministry team with two economists and spreadsheets — is precise. He has been in that room. He is not the ministry side, but he has worked with ministries that were. He does not challenge the framing. He settles in.

**Eleni** says nothing. Her posture is evaluative. She is checking whether the setup matches what she has seen in real programme negotiations. The reference to the factory worker in Amman whose operating budget is being reduced lands — she has worked on conditionality packages that produced exactly that outcome, and she has spent years working through the methodology in rooms that did not have adequate tools.

**Aicha** registers immediately. The framing of the minister sitting across from the IMF team is her professional reality. She does not need to be convinced this problem exists.

### Section 2 — Live Application

**Frame C — Thesis Frame (Step 3, 2026)**

*Presenter shows Frame C first per the presentation sequence.*

**Lucas** looks at the alert panel immediately. He sees two alerts: one TERMINAL (threshold crossed) on the governance axis, one CRITICAL on financial. He reads: "planetary boundary co2 proximity" and "democratic quality score." He looks puzzled — he expected reserve-related alerts at step 3 (2026). He says nothing yet.

**Aicha** sees "TERMINAL (threshold crossed)" and reads it literally: a threshold was crossed. She does not know which threshold, for which country, or what it means for the ministry's options. She waits for the presenter to explain.

> *Presenter narrates Step 3 Synthesis: "Two alert states. Two entirely different policy problems. Jordan's ministry needs to extend the reserve runway — Egypt's ministry is operating in governance breach. The Exploratory confidence tier on the governance alert means the direction is calibrated to V-Dem historical data. The magnitude is pending validation. The instrument names what it does not yet know."*

**Aicha** responds: "The label says 'Early estimate — confirm before citing.' I can work with that. It means I take the direction to the room and caveat the magnitude. That is a precise instruction. I have used less honest analytical tools."

**Eleni** has a question: "Egypt's governance score — is this from V-Dem 2023, or is it an assumption in the scenario? Because if Egypt's democratic quality was actually 0.07 in 2023, this is not a modelling claim — it is an observation." The presenter confirms: V-Dem 2023 data, pre-existing structural condition, not a scenario output. Eleni: "Then this is not an 'Early estimate.' This is a fact. The governance starting point is a fact. The Exploratory tier applies to the forward projection, not the baseline reading." This is a calibration gap observation — noted.

**Frame A — Instrument Cluster (Step 1, 2024)**

*Presenter returns to step 1 to show the instrument state at scenario load.*

**Lucas** sees the trajectory chart. He looks at the legend: four framework curves. He looks for entity labels — which curve belongs to Jordan, which to Egypt. He notes: "The legend shows '(JOR · EGY)' on each framework. That means both entities contribute to each curve. But I still cannot tell which entity is the primary driver of the Financial composite at step 1." He files this mentally as a methodology question for Q&A.

**Eleni** has a practical question mid-step: "Where do I see Jordan's reserve coverage? The presenter says 7.1 months at step 1. I cannot find that number on the screen." The presenter points to the alert panel — the FIN alert text references reserve_coverage_months — but the value is in small text in the alert body, not displayed as a standalone metric. Eleni: "That is a gap. The finding is in the narration, not in the instrument."

**Frame B — CRITICAL Alerts (Step 2, 2025)**

*Presenter advances to step 2. Egypt's governance CRITICAL has been present since step 1. Jordan's financial trajectory is declining.*

**Lucas** reads the alert text carefully. He is checking the indicator name, the severity classification, and the step index. He is satisfied that the alert text is technically accurate. He is building the methodology case mentally — he will need to check the threshold values against IMF standards before citing.

**Aicha** asks: "The trajectory — four lines. You said Jordan and Egypt. Which line is Jordan?" The presenter explains the framework composite architecture: each line is a framework, not an entity. Both JOR and EGY contribute to each composite. "The Financial curve is primarily driven by Jordan's reserve stress. The Governance curve is primarily driven by Egypt's governance breach. They are not separate lines — they are simultaneous analytical dimensions." Aicha: "I understand the architecture. But for a presentation to my board, I need to be able to point at a line and say 'this is Jordan.' Can that be done?" Answer: not in the current interface — filed as #845.

**Steps 3–7 — Baseline Advance**

*Presenter advances through steps 3–7, pausing at step 5.*

At step 5: "Reserve_coverage_months: 2.5. The CRITICAL floor." Lucas tracks the unemployment reversal carefully. The GCC support at step 3 (one-step lag: step 4 improvement 17.73%→16.59%). Conditionality at step 4 (one-step lag: step 5 reversal 16.59%→17.25%). Lucas: "That's the austerity paradox. The external support improved conditions. The conditionality reversed most of the gain within a single step. If that dynamic is consistent with the model's fiscal multiplier assumptions, that is a citable finding."

By step 7: three TERMINAL alerts visible simultaneously. Eleni, quietly: "And that is the baseline. Reserves at zero. Three simultaneous threshold crossings. The ministry had eight steps to prevent that. The tool shows you the path."

**Frame D — Mode 3 Active Control (Branch from Step 3, 1.30×)**

*Presenter activates Mode 3 and sets fiscal multiplier to 1.30.*

**Aicha** asks: "1.30 — what does that mean in plain language?" Presenter: GCC emergency support multiplies the government's spending capacity 30% more effectively than the baseline IMF-conditionality structure. Aicha: "And the result?"

Presenter reads the Step 6 synthesis: "+1.70pp GDP at step 5. Unemployment does not reverse. Reserve curve: identical in baseline and branch — both reach zero by step 7."

**Aicha**, immediately: "The reserves run out either way." Presenter: "Correct. The reserve crisis is survived under better conditions, not avoided. Better conditionality terms improve GDP and employment trajectories. They do not change Jordan's structural import dependency." Aicha: "That is the most important thing you have said. If I walk into a room and say 'accept the liquidity without the spending cut,' the response will be 'it doesn't change the reserve outcome.' I need to know that in advance. The fact that the tool says it clearly — before the negotiation — is the capability."

**Lucas** notes the fiscal multiplier value: "1.30 is at the upper end of the peer-reviewed range for Jordan. The Blanchard-Leigh range for similar economies is 0.8–1.4. If you ran this at 0.9 and 1.1, would the unemployment divergence still appear?" This is a sensitivity analysis question — the tool supports it via Mode 3 re-runs, but the presenter does not demonstrate this in the session.

**Frame E — All Four Axes (Step 5, 2028)**

*Presenter returns to step 5, all four composite scores visible.*

**PRESENTER BEAT — reading confidence tier label aloud:**

> Presenter points to the GOV alert in Zone 1B. Reads: "Early estimate — confirm before citing." Explains: "That is the instrument naming what it does not yet know. Egypt's governance deterioration is calibrated to V-Dem historical patterns. The magnitude — the rate of deterioration — is the Exploratory component. The ministry team sees this label before they cite the finding at the table."

**Lucas** responds: "That is the right framing. The tier labels are actionable. What data would I provide to move the GOV finding from Exploratory to Moderate? V-Dem disaggregated by year? An IMF governance review?" Presenter: this is the M13 calibration workflow — the tier upgrade path is the mechanism the ministry team would use to validate and cite.

**Aicha** registers the confidence tier explanation. She does not ask a follow-up. She is processing.

**Step 7 SYNTHESIS — Exploratory callback and four-axis claim:**

> *Presenter delivers Step 7 SYNTHESIS opening:* "Returning to the Exploratory disclosure from Step 4: Egypt's governance alert carried that label then, and it holds now in the four-axis view. The direction of deterioration is calibrated to V-Dem historical patterns. The magnitude is pending further validation. When the ministry team runs this scenario with their own calibration data, the Exploratory label is the honest marker of exactly what they need to provide. The instrument does not hide what it does not know — it names it, so the ministry team can act on it."

**Aicha** says nothing. She is listening.

> *Presenter delivers the Greece 2014 synthesis.*

**Eleni**, after a pause: "Greece 2014. GDP said recovery. We were in those rooms. The programme was technically on track on every metric we were measuring. It was not on track on anything that mattered to the people it affected. I do not know if WorldSim would have changed the outcome in 2012. But I know that the four-axis view would have changed the argument."

### Section 3 — Backtesting Credibility

**Lucas** is fully engaged here. He is checking the methodology at each claim.

On Argentina: "Simulated contraction −10.55%. Historical −10.9%. Deviation 3.2%. If this holds across two independent cases, I would cite the engine in a staff report." He notes: "You said remaining four cases are DIRECTION_ONLY. That is honest. Do not claim more."

On Greece: "What is the basis for the threshold calibration on reserve_coverage_months? The IMF minimum adequate reserve benchmark — is that the ARA metric, the import coverage standard, or Jordan-specific?" This is a precise methodology question. The presenter references the honest disclosures section. Lucas accepts that it is disclosed.

On Ecuador as a recovery case: "That is the right test. If the engine only validates during contraction, it is not a balanced tool. Ecuador shows it can distinguish stabilization from continued decline."

**Aicha** becomes more engaged here. She is listening for the political feasibility gap specifically.

## Stakeholder Assessment

**Overall reaction:** Positive — with precision. None of the three audience proxies challenged the mission framing or the core analytical claims. The technical engagement was serious: Lucas tested the backtesting claims carefully, Eleni probed the conditionality mechanics, Aicha engaged most directly with the decision-making utility.

**Most compelling moment:**

Aicha's response to the Mode 3 reserve curve convergence — "The reserves run out either way" — followed immediately by: "That is the most important thing you have said. The fact that the tool says it clearly, before the negotiation, is the capability."

This is the moment the demo is designed to produce. The minister does not need the tool to give her the answer she wants. She needs it to give her the answer that is true, early enough to prepare the argument.

**Most questioned element:**

The absence of per-entity curves in the trajectory view. Both Eleni and Aicha independently asked the same question — "which line is Jordan?" — at different points in the session. The legend label `(JOR · EGY)` resolves the ambiguity partially but does not satisfy the need to point at a single line and name an entity. This is the most persistent UX gap in the demo.

The reserve_coverage_months visibility gap was a close second — Aicha asked "where do I see 2.5 months on the screen?" directly and the answer was unsatisfying.

## North Star Test — Stakeholder Version

**North star question:** Does this demo change what a finance minister's team could argue at the table in a live negotiation?

**Aicha Mbaye's verdict (Persona 5 — Finance Minister proxy):**

> "What I take from this is: Jordan cannot negotiate its way out of a reserve crisis — reserves run out by 2030 whether we accept the conditionality or not. But the question at the table is who absorbs the employment cost of the spending cut. This tool shows that accepting the GCC liquidity without the conditionality produces 1.7 percentage points more GDP and protects employment at step 5. That argument is now quantified, not just a position. That is what I would bring into a negotiation."

**Verdict: PASS.**

The finding is specific (not aspirational), names the scenario (Jordan, Hormuz disruption, step 3 branch), names the finding (1.7pp GDP, employment protection, reserve curve unchanged), and is stated without specialist mediation.

**What Aicha could argue at the table that she could not argue before:**

"We ran the scenario. IMF liquidity without the spending cut produces better employment outcomes. The reserve crisis happens either way — we are not claiming otherwise. We are saying the cost of the spending cut falls on Jordanian workers, not on the reserve trajectory. That is the negotiating argument. We have the numbers."
