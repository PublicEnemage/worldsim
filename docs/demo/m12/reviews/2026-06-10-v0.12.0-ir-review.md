## Preliminary Observations

Before entering the structured review: Frames A, B, and C are rendered at severely reduced
resolution — the instrument cluster in Zone 1A is essentially illegible at viewing size. Frame
D and Frame E are sharper and substantially more readable. This asymmetry affects what can be
confirmed from the visuals versus what must be inferred from application state. This asymmetry
is itself a finding (DEMO-042/043).

## Part 1 — Narration vs. Visual Match

### Frame C — Thesis Frame (Step 3, 2026 — Divergence)

The walkthrough designates Frame C as the opening thesis frame in the C → A → B → D → E
sequence. The narration for Steps 3 and 4 makes the following specific claims:

- Zone 1A shows JOR reserve coverage at 5.0 months, declining at 1.2 months/step
- Zone 1A shows EGY governance curve in sustained breach
- Zone 1B MDA alert panel is visible and readable
- Step label reads 3/8, 2026

**What the visual shows:** The step header reads "Step 2 / 8" — this appears to be step 2,
not step 3. If correct, the narration's claims about step 3 data (5.0 month reserve coverage,
1.2 months/step burn rate, governance decline toward 0.03) cannot be read from this frame.
The instrument cluster in Zone 1A is entirely unreadable at demo scale — trajectory curves
are present as colored lines but no values, labels, or step markers are legible. The MDA alert
panel shows alert badges (TERMINAL and CRITICAL entries visible as colored blocks) but text
within them is too small to read. The choropleth map dominates the lower two-thirds of the screen.

**Finding:** The step counter visible in the header contradicts the narration. The thesis
frame is labeled step 2. Either the screenshot was captured at the wrong step, or the walkthrough
was updated to reference step 3 after the screenshot was captured. This is the first frame the
audience sees — presenting the analytical argument with the wrong step visible would be
immediately noticed by any domain expert counting along.

**Finding on map proportion:** The narration explicitly states "Do not narrate the choropleth
as the analytical argument. The choropleth is context." But the choropleth occupies approximately
60% of the visible screen area. The instrument cluster is in the top third at a size where no
specific value is legible. The visual hierarchy contradicts the narration's stated priority.

## Part 2 — Domain Expert UX Feedback

### Zone 1A — Trajectory View

I cannot read the trajectory view without a specialist mediating in Frames A, B, and C. The
instrument cluster is rendered in the top 25–30% of the screen; the map occupies the remainder.
Trajectory curves are present as colored lines but carry no legible labels, step values, or
threshold lines. I can see curves and that they are roughly declining, but I cannot independently
cite "Jordan reserve coverage at 5.0 months at step 3" — I would have to take the presenter's
word for it.

Frame E is substantially better. Trajectory curves are readable, step labels on the x-axis
visible (Step 0 Dec 2023 through Step 5 Dec 2027), and threshold lines present. But even in
Frame E, individual entity curves (JOR vs. EGY) are not labeled inline. I cannot tell which
colored line is Jordan and which is Egypt without reading the legend at the bottom in small text.
In a negotiation room, I need to point at a screen and say "that curve is Jordan." I cannot do
that from these screenshots.

### Zone 1B — MDA Alert Panel

In Frames A, B, and C: alert badges are visible as colored blocks (TERMINAL red, CRITICAL orange)
but text — the specific indicator, step, finding, confidence tier — is not legible. This means
the entire function of the MDA alert panel is not deliverable from these frames at normal
presentation scale.

In Frame E: alerts are legible. TERMINAL ECO and TERMINAL GOV entries are readable. The
"Moderate confidence — cite with caveat" and "Exploratory — do not cite" confidence labels
are visible and will draw sophisticated-audience attention immediately.

The "Exploratory — do not cite" on the central governance finding is a serious UX problem
for this demo. The alert is TERMINAL severity — the most prominent visual classification —
and simultaneously carries a "do not cite" research confidence label. These two signals conflict.
A domain expert reads: this is the most serious finding (TERMINAL) but you cannot use it
(do not cite). For a policy analyst evaluating analytical credibility, this combination is
damaging.

### Zone 1D — Four-Framework Composite Scores

In Frame E, the composite score panel is legible and I can read all four values. However, I
cannot explain what a score of 0.37 on Governance means without a reference. The composite score
0.37 is visible, but there is no floor reference on the panel itself — I do not know from the
visual whether 0.37 is above or below the governance floor. The MDA alerts tell me it is in
breach, but the relationship between the composite score and the floor threshold is not visually
communicated.

For a non-specialist finance minister I am briefing: I can say "governance scores 0.37 out of
a possible 1.0" but I cannot say "this means we are below the critical threshold" from the
visual alone. The information is in the alert panel, not on the composite score widget.

### Mode 3 Comparison (Frames D → E)

In Frame D I can see that a branch exists and the Active Control panel is configured (1.30×,
branch from step 3, "Apply Change" executed). In Frame E I see four-framework scores and
trajectory curves with "Branched from step 3 — baseline locked."

What I cannot do: I cannot read the two-trajectory comparison in Zone 1A and cite "the branch
produces X% improvement in unemployment at step 5 versus the baseline." The trajectory curves
are present but comparative values are not labeled. The narration states specific figures
("+0.68pp GDP at step 4," "+1.70pp GDP at step 5"). Neither figure appears anywhere in the
visual output. A domain economist will ask "where does that number come from?" and the answer
is "the presenter stated it" not "the instrument shows it."

### Potential embarrassment in front of a sophisticated audience

Three elements would draw scrutiny from a sophisticated audience:

1. The "Exploratory — do not cite" label on the TERMINAL GOV alert in Frame E. A monetary
   economist or policy analyst who has worked with research confidence classifications will
   read this immediately and raise questions about the entire methodology.

2. Any step counter mismatch between the narration and the screenshots. A participant following
   along or a skeptical technical advisor counting steps will note the discrepancy immediately.

3. The Human Development composite shows "Irreversible." This is the strongest analytical
   claim in the demo. If it is solid, it is also the most powerful. If it is an artifact of
   model parameters, it will be challenged hard.

## Part 4 — Root Cause Analysis

### Shared Root Cause A — Screenshots captured at wrong step state
**Explains: DEMO-039**

Frame C (thesis frame) shows step 2/8 in the header when the walkthrough specifies step 3.
The screenshots were either captured from the same session state before step advancement was
complete, or the walkthrough was revised after screenshots were captured. This single error
means the opening frame of the demo shows incorrect step data.

### Shared Root Cause B — Instrument not scaled for projection
**Explains: DEMO-042, DEMO-043, DEMO-044, DEMO-051**

The application's layout is designed for a desktop user working close to a 1440×900 monitor.
The instrument cluster, alert panel, and trajectory curve annotations are sized for individual
use — hovering, scrolling, reading at proximity. In a presentation context (projector, room
display, shared screen), the effective reading distance and resolution both work against
legibility. The fix is not to resize text globally — it is to implement a presentation/demo
mode that expands the instrument cluster and suppresses the map (or reduces it to a thumbnail)
when analytical steps are being narrated. This is the issue that explains four HIGH findings
from a single root cause.

### Shared Root Cause C — Confidence tier design creates credibility collision at TERMINAL level
**Explains: DEMO-041, DEMO-047**

The confidence tier system is correctly assigning "Exploratory — do not cite" to a methodology
not yet validated against backtesting. But the collision between TERMINAL severity (most alarming
visual classification) and "do not cite" (most restrictive citation confidence) creates a UX
failure at the worst possible moment. The same system issue affects the ecological composite —
the composite looks complete and carries "Moderate confidence — cite with caveat," but the
CO2-only limitation is invisible. Confidence tiers were designed to inform analysts in the full
application, not to coexist with the severity classification system in a demo context where a
sophisticated audience reads both signals simultaneously.

## Single Most Important Finding

**DEMO-041: "Exploratory — do not cite" on the TERMINAL GOV alert in Frame E.**

DEMO-039 (wrong step counter) is immediately fixable by recapturing the screenshot — a process
error, not a capability gap. A day of work resolves it.

DEMO-042/043/044 (legibility at scale) are real UX gaps but a presenter can compensate by
narrating through them and using a laser pointer. Experienced presenters handle this regularly.

DEMO-041 cannot be compensated by narration skill. The moment a domain economist in that room
reads "Exploratory — do not cite" on the TERMINAL governance finding, the entire evidential
argument for two-country simultaneous modelling is compromised. The governance divergence between
Jordan and Egypt is the analytical centrepiece of this demo — the thing that makes the two-country
approach legible as a claim, not just a feature. If that finding is methodologically uncitable,
a sophisticated audience will question every other alert and every other confidence tier in the
instrument. The damage is not contained to the governance axis. It spreads.

The fix options are not simple: either (a) the governance methodology needs to be validated to
a "cite with caveat" level before this demo runs to a real stakeholder audience, or (b) the
narration must get ahead of it explicitly — the presenter names the "Exploratory — do not cite"
label, explains what it means and why it is there, and reframes it as evidence of methodological
honesty rather than a credibility gap. Option (b) is manageable and is consistent with the
