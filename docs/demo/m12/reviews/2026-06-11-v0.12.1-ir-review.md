## Part 2 — Domain Expert UX Feedback

### On the thesis frame (Frame C) as the opening argument

I am a senior policy analyst. You have shown me a slide that leads with a world map
and a collection of colored lines in the upper quarter that I cannot read without a
magnifying glass. The header says "Step 2 / 8." Your narration says "step 3, 2026,
dual shock peak." I am already skeptical before your presenter has finished their
first sentence.

The analytical argument — two countries, same shock, different failure modes — is
correct and compelling. The opening frame does not deliver it. The thesis is in the
presenter's words, not in the visual. In a negotiation room I need evidence I can
point to. I cannot point to anything readable in this frame.

### On the alert panel as a citeable finding

In Frames A, B, C: I cannot read the MDA alert panel. I know alerts are firing
because I can see colored badges. I cannot read the indicator name, the step, the
severity text, or the confidence tier. In a room where I am trying to build credibility
with a technically sophisticated counterpart, I cannot cite findings I have read
aloud from the presenter. I need to be able to read them myself from the instrument.

In Frame E: I can read the alerts. The TERMINAL ECO finding says "Moderate confidence —
cite with caveat." The TERMINAL GOV finding says "Exploratory — do not cite." I have
15 years of experience working with research confidence classifications. "Exploratory —
do not cite" on a TERMINAL governance finding means the governance breach is the most
serious analytical classification the tool assigns, but the methodology producing it
has not been validated to a level where I can use it in a negotiation. These two signals
are in direct conflict. In a room with IMF economists across the table, I cannot cite
an "Exploratory" finding as the basis for a counter-proposal without being immediately
challenged on methodology. If I have to pre-emptively disclaim it, the TERMINAL label
loses its force. This is the most damaging finding in the demo.

### On the PMM at step 5

The Policy Maneuver Margin shows 1.00 → at the step the narration describes as
"most compressed." I do not know what the PMM scale means from the instrument alone.
If 1.00 means maximum margin, the narration is wrong. If 1.00 means the margin is at
its historical boundary and the right arrow means "deteriorating," the instrument is
not communicating that reading without a mediator. A finance ministry official who
picks up this instrument and looks at "1.00 →" during a crisis scenario would not
read "compressed margin." They would read "at capacity" or "normal." This needs
to be either recalibrated or re-labeled.

### On the Mode 3 branch comparison (Frames D and E)

The Mode 3 Active Control is the most significant M12 delivery. The functional claim
is that a finance minister can branch from step 3 and see what the GCC emergency
support scenario produces versus the baseline.

What I can see: that Mode 3 is active (1.30× multiplier, branch from step 3, baseline
locked). What I cannot see: any quantitative statement of what the branch produces
differently from the baseline. The narration states "+0.68pp GDP at step 4" and
"+1.70pp GDP at step 5" as the branch consequences. Neither value appears anywhere on
screen. In a negotiation room, "the branch shows a divergence" is not a citable finding.
"+1.70pp GDP improvement at step 5 under the negotiated conditionality scenario" is.
The instrument as captured does not deliver the citable output that Mode 3 was designed
to produce.

### On the four-framework composite panel as a capability claim

The narration closes on Zone 1D as evidence of M12's capability: "all four composite
scores simultaneously live, the first time in demo history." That capability claim is
real and the four scores are indeed non-null. But the panel shows single values per
axis, not JOR vs EGY comparisons. The analytical argument for two-country simultaneous
modelling — that the same shock produces different financial vs governance failures
in the two countries — should be visible in Zone 1D as diverging per-entity values.
If the panel shows only one entity's composites, the two-country argument is only in
the trajectory view, not in the closing capability frame.

Additionally: a Governance score of 0.37 is uninterpretable without a floor reference.
I can see from the TERMINAL GOV alert that this is in breach. But the composite panel
itself does not show where the floor is. As a finance ministry official being briefed
by the presenter, I need the instrument to tell me whether 0.37 is a little below the
floor or catastrophically below it. Currently I am dependent on the alert panel for
that context, which is not visible on the composite panel itself.

## Part 4 — Root Cause Analysis

### Shared Root Cause A — Screenshot capture protocol not enforced
**Explains: DEMO-056, DEMO-058**

Frame C (thesis frame) shows step 2 — identical to the DEMO-039 finding in the prior
review. Either the prior finding was not fixed before new screenshots were captured,
or the capture process does not include a step-verification checkpoint. Frame D shows
what appears to be a full-trajectory display inconsistent with step-3 branching. Both
findings point to a screenshot capture process that does not verify application state
before capturing — the screenshots are taken from whatever state the application is in,
not from a specified, verified state.

A pre-capture checklist exists in the screenshot brief (six items including step
verification, MDA alert state, and Mode 3 toggle state). The evidence from the
screenshots suggests this checklist is not being enforced. The same error (wrong step
in Frame C) appearing in two consecutive reviews is the indicator.

### Shared Root Cause B — Instrument not scaled for presentation
**Explains: DEMO-061, DEMO-063, DEMO-069**

The application's layout is optimized for a desktop user at close range. The instrument
cluster, alert panel, and trajectory curves are sized for individual analysis — hovering,
scrolling, reading at proximity. In a presentation context, the effective resolution
disadvantages the instrument relative to the map. Three findings in this review trace
to the same underlying layout: no presentation mode that expands the instrument and
reduces the map during analytical steps. This was Shared Root Cause B in the prior
review and has not been addressed.

### Shared Root Cause C — Confidence tier and severity classification create credibility 
collision at TERMINAL level
**Explains: DEMO-057, DEMO-066**

The confidence tier system correctly assigns "Exploratory — do not cite" to the governance
composite methodology, which has not been validated against backtesting cases. But the
collision of TERMINAL severity (most alarming visual class) with "do not cite" (most
restrictive citation confidence) is a UX failure specifically in demo contexts where a
sophisticated audience reads both signals simultaneously. Similarly, the ecological
composite appears complete when it is CO2-only — the confidence tier says "Moderate
confidence — cite with caveat" without disclosing the coverage limitation. Both findings
reflect a confidence tier display system designed for analyst-mode individual use, not
for stakeholder demo projection.

### Shared Root Cause D — Mode 3 branch function lacks comparative output layer
**Explains: DEMO-059 (partially), DEMO-064**

Mode 3 is implemented (branch anchor, multiplier, trajectory recompute) but the output
layer that would make the branch citable is not: no step-level comparison readout, no
labeled delta values, and the PMM does not clearly communicate compressed margin in a
branching state. The counter-proposal function is structurally present but analytically
incomplete — the instrument can branch but cannot produce the quantitative output a
finance minister would need to cite at a table.

### Shared Root Cause E — Prior review findings not tracked to resolution
**Explains: DEMO-056 (= DEMO-039), DEMO-057 (= DEMO-041), DEMO-061 (= DEMO-042/043),
DEMO-063 (= DEMO-044), DEMO-064 (= DEMO-045), DEMO-065 (= DEMO-049), DEMO-066 (=
DEMO-047), DEMO-067 (= DEMO-048), DEMO-068 (= DEMO-050), DEMO-069 (= DEMO-051)**

Ten of fourteen findings in this review are carried forward from the prior review
unchanged. Three of the four CRITICAL findings in this review were CRITICAL findings
in the prior review (DEMO-039, DEMO-041, both unresolved). The fixes filed as
DEMO-052 through DEMO-055 resolved four issues; the remaining ten did not appear in

full issue list warranted — either time constraints limited the fix scope, or the

v0.12.1 screenshots to a stakeholder audience with ten unresolved prior findings —
including two unresolved CRITICAL findings — represents a gap in the pre-demo
quality gate.

## Single Most Important Finding

**DEMO-057: "Exploratory — do not cite" on the TERMINAL GOV alert in Frame E remains
unresolved from the prior review.**

Every other finding in this review is either a capture process failure (DEMO-056,
DEMO-058 — fixable by recapturing screenshots), a legibility issue a presenter can
partially compensate for (DEMO-061, DEMO-063), or a feature gap that reduces the
demo's analytical power without actively undermining its credibility (DEMO-059,
DEMO-064). DEMO-057 is different in kind.

The governance breach in Egypt is the analytical centrepiece of the two-country
argument. It is the finding that separates "Jordan has reserve stress" from "two
countries, same shock, two entirely different failure mechanisms, two entirely
different policy responses required." Without the governance finding, the demo
demonstrates a commodity shock with a financial impact — which is not novel. With
the governance finding, the demo demonstrates that commodity shocks can simultaneously
produce financial crises in one country and governance deterioration in another — which
is the WorldSim analytical claim.

When a senior policy analyst reads "TERMINAL — democratic quality score — all cohorts
— Exploratory — do not cite" in the closing frame of the demo, the natural inference
is that the two-country analytical argument rests on a finding the tool itself says
cannot be cited. In a room where the goal is to establish that WorldSim gives the
ministry analytical standing comparable to their counterparts — the capability to
bring a citable finding to the table — "do not cite" in the final frame is the
opposite of that promise.

The fix options are two:
1. Validate the governance methodology to "Cite with caveat" before the stakeholder
   demo. This requires a backtesting case with a known governance outcome against
   which the governance composite pathway can be tested.
2. Add explicit narration to the walkthrough at Step 4 (before Frame E is shown)
   that names the "Exploratory" label, explains what it means, and reframes it as
   evidence of methodological honesty: "The label says 'Exploratory — do not cite'
   because the governance methodology has not yet been backtested against a case with
   a measured governance outcome. We are showing you what the model produces; we are
   also showing you the confidence tier. These two pieces of information together are
   the honest output. We will not hide the confidence tier to make the finding look
   stronger than it is."

Option 2 is achievable before the stakeholder demo without additional implementation
work. Option 1 is the correct long-term fix. Either must be executed before this demo
runs to a domain expert audience. The current walkthrough does not prepare the
presenter for this moment, and the current screenshots guarantee the moment will come.
