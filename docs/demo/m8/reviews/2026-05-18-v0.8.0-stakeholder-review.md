## Executive Summary

The M8 demo represents a material advance over the M6 baseline: MDA alerts fire and are visible, the radar chart renders with real data on two live axes, the ecological dimension is present for the first time, and the governance axis renders honest null rather than zero. The demo also runs end-to-end without errors. These are genuine improvements.

However, five findings at HIGH severity or above indicate the demo is not yet ready for a first-impression stakeholder session. The most consequential is the choropleth: the "watch Greece shift" promise that the presenter makes in steps 1, 2, and 3 is not supported by any visible change in the map across the five frames. For a sophisticated analyst, a map that doesn't move when the narration says it is moving is worse than no map — it raises the question of whether the tool's outputs are connected to its display. The second critical gap is drawer legibility: the MDA alerts, radar axis labels, and ecological values require the audience to squint or trust the narrator, rather than being independently readable. At a conference table or on a projected screen, the finding that is supposed to be "specific enough to cite in a negotiation" cannot be cited if it cannot be read.

The three root causes identified below explain seven of the nine findings. Resolving Root Cause B (drawer panel density) would close the most high-severity findings and has the largest single impact on demo credibility.

### DEMO-001 — Choropleth shows no visible change in Greece across six simulation steps

- **Severity:** 🔴 Critical
- **Frames:** A (Step 1), B (Step 3), C (Step 5) — map visible in all three
- **Narration (Step 1 of presenter guide):**
  > "Each step is one year. Watch Greece shift in the choropleth as the fiscal contraction accumulates across steps."
- **Screen:** The choropleth map in Frames A, B, and C is visually indistinguishable. The country boundaries are the same, the color distribution is the same, and Greece is not distinctively colored relative to neighboring countries at any of the three steps. The attribute selector reads `gdp_growth [...]` (truncated), confirming the correct attribute is active. No visible shift occurs at any step.
- **Credibility impact:** This is the central live demonstration claim — the scenario is running, the country is responding, and you can see it. If the map does not change when the narration says it is changing, two interpretations are available to the audience: the model is not producing meaningful output, or the tool's display is not connected to its outputs. Neither interpretation is acceptable in a first-impression session. For a domain analyst who has seen economic data visualizations, a choropleth that reads as static across three years of documented fiscal contraction raises a specific suspicion: the scenario has no real inputs. The M6 review (DEMO-004) identified exactly this failure mode, and the fix at M8 was to inject Greece programme inputs — but the visual evidence of those inputs is not surfacing on the map.
- **Root cause hypothesis:** The `gdp_growth` attribute for a single-entity scenario (only GRC) is normalized against a global distribution of ~200 countries. Greece's change, large in absolute terms, occupies a narrow band in the global color scale and maps to an indistinguishable shift. The choropleth is measuring the right thing but displaying it at a scale where the signal is invisible. A scenario-relative or regional color scale would make the change legible.
- **Recommended fix:** For single-entity or small-set scenarios, apply a scenario-relative color scale that normalizes against the entity's own range across steps rather than the global country distribution. Alternatively, display an explicit delta indicator — "GRC: −5.4% → −8.9% → −7.1%" — in a heads-up overlay for the entity in the active scenario so the change is legible regardless of global scale compression.
- **GitHub Issue:** To be filed

### DEMO-002 — Thesis frame (Frame C) does not self-demonstrate the core claim without narration

- **Severity:** 🔴 Critical
- **Frames:** C (Step 5, shown first as the thesis frame)
- **Narration (Step 8 of presenter guide):**
  > "Step 5 is 2014. In the historical record, Greece's GDP grew 0.7 percent — a financial recovery. But unemployment was still 26.5 percent. Look at the radar: the financial axis is extending as the model registers partial recovery. The human development axis remains near its most depressed point. This asymmetry is the WorldSim argument in a single image. Financial recovery and human recovery are not the same event. No single-axis measurement tool can show you both simultaneously."
- **Screen:** Frame C shows the drawer open on the Human Development tab with a table of indicators. The radar chart is visible at the top of the drawer but the axis labels are not readable at screenshot resolution. The map background shows the same static choropleth as all other frames.
- **Credibility impact:** This frame is shown first — it is the thesis before the evidence. The WorldSim argument is that financial recovery and human recovery are not the same event, and that a multi-framework instrument can show both simultaneously. For this to land as a self-evident image, the radar asymmetry must be visually obvious: one axis extending (financial) while another remains compressed (human development). If the radar is present but axis labels are unreadable and the asymmetric deformation is not legible at projection scale, the thesis is thesis-by-narration only. A domain analyst who saw Frame C without a presenter would not be able to read "financial recovery ≠ human development recovery" from the chart unassisted. The argument collapses from evidence to assertion the moment the narrator leaves the room.
- **Root cause hypothesis:** The radar chart is rendered at a size that places it in the upper portion of a narrow drawer panel. Axis labels and score values are not legible at the display scale used for screenshot capture. The deformation is likely present in the SVG geometry but is not perceivable without zooming.
- **Recommended fix:** The radar chart is the thesis visualization — it must be legible at demo presentation scale without zooming. Minimum requirements: axis labels readable at 12pt equivalent, each axis arm labeled with its score value, the Governance dashed axis visually distinct. Consider a standalone radar view (full-screen or modal) accessible from the drawer for demo contexts, so the thesis image can fill a presentation slide rather than occupy one-quarter of a narrow panel.
- **GitHub Issue:** To be filed

### DEMO-003 — MDA alert panel: finding text not readable at demo scale

- **Severity:** 🟠 Significant
- **Frames:** D (Step 3, MDA panel prominent), A (Step 1)
- **Narration (presenter guide §Step 5):**
  > "Read the alert as evidence: indicator / step / severity / cohort."
- **Screen:** Two red MDA alert banners are visible in Frames A and D: `MDA-FIN-RESERVES` and `MDA-ECO-CO2-BOUNDARY`. The headers are legible. The body text of each alert — which is supposed to contain the indicator, step, severity, and cohort — is too small to read at screenshot resolution.
- **Credibility impact:** The presenter instructs the audience to read the alert as evidence — a finding specific enough to cite in a negotiation. That instruction only works if the audience can perform the reading. In a conference room projection at 30 feet, or in a screenshot sent ahead of a meeting, body text that requires zooming is body text that will not be read. A domain analyst cannot cite a finding they cannot read. The structural design of the MDA alert (red banner, header identifier, body text) is correct and matches the framing. The failure is font size and panel width.
- **Root cause hypothesis:** Root Cause B — drawer panel too narrow and dense for its analytical purpose. The drawer occupies approximately 25% of the viewport and contains the radar chart, MDA panel, PMM indicator, and framework tabs stacked vertically. At this proportion, body text in the MDA panel renders below readable threshold.
- **Recommended fix:** The MDA alert body text — specifically the indicator name, breach step, severity level, and affected cohort — should render at a minimum of 13pt equivalent and must be legible without zooming at 1440×900 resolution. Consider extracting the MDA panel to a more prominent Zone 1 position with more horizontal space, or providing a full-panel view when alerts are active.
- **GitHub Issue:** To be filed

### DEMO-004 — MDA alerts appear identical across steps 1 through 5; findings do not accumulate

- **Severity:** 🟠 Significant
- **Frames:** A (Step 1), D and B (Step 3)
- **Narration (presenter guide §Step 5):**
  > "Each step is one year. Watch Greece shift... These are Minimum Descent Altitude alerts — levels below which consequences become irreversible."
- **Screen:** The same two alerts (`MDA-FIN-RESERVES`, `MDA-ECO-CO2-BOUNDARY`) fire at Step 1 and appear visually identical in the Step 3 frames. There is no visible indication that alert severity has changed, deepened, or that new alerts have been added across the three visible steps.
- **Credibility impact:** The MDA system's analytical value is temporal: it should show at what programme step a threshold was crossed and how the situation evolves. An alert that fires at step 1 and looks identical at step 3 and step 5 reads as a static warning, not an accumulating consequence tracker. A static warning says "this condition exists." An accumulating finding says "this threshold was crossed at step 2 under these conditions and deepened at step 4." The latter is a finding you can cite. The former is a dashboard light. The distinction is the difference between a useful negotiation tool and an alarm system, and the narration explicitly promises the former while the display may be delivering the latter.
- **Root cause hypothesis:** Root Cause C — the MDA alert panel may be displaying all alerts that have ever fired in the scenario run, rather than alerts scoped to the current step or showing the first-breach step prominently. If this is a display issue (filtering logic), the fix is straightforward. If alerts genuinely fire at step 1 and remain unchanged through step 5 for this scenario, the alert calibration for the Greece programme inputs may need review.
- **Recommended fix:** The MDA alert body must prominently display the step at which the threshold was first crossed. If alert state changes between steps (severity increases, new alerts fire, existing alerts deepen), this change must be visually distinguishable between frames — e.g., a badge showing "first breach: step 2" and "current severity: step 5 — deepened." An audience watching the step counter increment should be able to see the alert state update.
- **GitHub Issue:** To be filed

  > "Governance shows as a dashed axis, labeled 'Governance — in validation.' It renders honest null — not zero. Zero would imply governance failure. Null means the composite is not yet computed."

The honest-null design decision — which is one of the most methodologically important choices in the M8 demo, demonstrating that the tool does not fabricate data to complete a display — communicates nothing to the audience from the screenshots.

- **Recommended fix:** At the current drawer scale, consider supplementing the dashed axis with a text annotation directly below the radar chart: "Governance — in validation (M9)" or a small legend item that makes the null explicit. ### DEMO-007 — Ecological boundary proximity value (1.8) not self-interpreting; reference point absent

- **Severity:** 🟡 Minor
- **Frames:** E (Ecological tab)
- **Narration (presenter guide §Step 6):**
  > "Ecological is now live as of Milestone 8 — CO2 planetary boundary proximity, boundary-normalized against 350 ppm."
- **Screen:** Frame E shows the Ecological tab active in the drawer with numerical values visible. The CO2 boundary proximity value appears to read approximately 1.8. No reference point showing where the boundary falls (1.0 = boundary, >1.0 = exceeded) is visible in the frame.
- **Credibility impact:** A value of 1.8 on a proximity metric has no meaning without knowing the reference. The Rockström 2009 framework normalizes values so that 1.0 = the planetary boundary. A value of 1.8 means the boundary is exceeded by 80%. This is a meaningful finding — but it is invisible to an audience that does not already know the normalization convention. For a finance ministry analyst without a background in planetary science, 1.8 reads as an arbitrary number, not a finding. The POLICY.md commits to outputs that are "self-interpreting for non-technical users." This one is not.
- **Root cause hypothesis:** The ecological module correctly computes the boundary-normalized value, but the UI renders it as a raw number without the reference-point context that gives it meaning. The display name registry (Issue #317 / PR #329) handles human-readable indicator labels, but does not appear to provide an inline reference value annotation.
- **Recommended fix:** Display the boundary reference point inline alongside the proximity value. Minimum: a label reading "CO2 Boundary Proximity: 1.8 (boundary = 1.0; exceeded)" or a small threshold line on the indicator row. This is a UI annotation change, not a data change.
- **GitHub Issue:** To be filed

## Root Cause Analysis

Three root causes explain seven of the nine findings.

---

**Root Cause A — Single-entity choropleth not legible at global scale (explains DEMO-001)**

The Greece scenario runs with a single entity (GRC). The `gdp_growth` choropleth normalizes color against a global distribution of ~200 countries. Greece's change, large relative to its own trajectory, occupies a narrow band in the global color scale and produces no visible color shift across six steps. This is not a data error — the outputs are correct — it is a display architecture mismatch: the global-normalization choropleth was designed for cross-country comparison, and it cannot serve as a single-entity trajectory display without modification. The fix is a scenario-relative color scale or an entity-specific overlay when a single-entity scenario is active.

This root cause is distinct from Root Cause A in the M6 review (which identified missing scheduled inputs as the reason for empty outputs). In M8, the scenario runs correctly and produces outputs — but those outputs are not visible in the map display format chosen.

---

**Root Cause B — Drawer panel too narrow and dense for its analytical purpose (explains DEMO-002, DEMO-003, DEMO-005, DEMO-006)**

The entity detail drawer occupies approximately 25% of the viewport width and contains, stacked vertically: the radar chart, MDA alert panel, PMM indicator, framework tab selector, and per-framework indicator tables. At this proportion and density, four distinct legibility failures occur simultaneously: the MDA alert body text is too small to read (DEMO-003), the radar axis labels and scores are unreadable (DEMO-005), the thesis asymmetry in the radar cannot be perceived without zooming (DEMO-002), and the governance honest-null dashed axis with its "in validation" label is invisible (DEMO-006).

These four findings are a single architectural problem, not four separate bugs. The drawer was designed for desktop use at close range and has not been validated for demo presentation contexts (projected screen, conference table distance, screenshot sharing). The fix is either architectural (wider drawer, standalone radar view, larger minimum font sizes in analytical surfaces) or presentational (a full-screen radar modal accessible from the drawer for demo contexts).

This is the highest-priority root cause by finding count and severity: resolving it is expected to close DEMO-002, DEMO-003, DEMO-005, and DEMO-006 simultaneously.

---

**Root Cause C — MDA alerts may be displaying all-run alerts rather than step-specific findings (explains DEMO-004)**

If the same two alerts appear at step 1 and remain visually identical through step 5, the alert panel is functioning as a static indicator rather than an accumulating consequence tracker. The most likely explanation is that the panel renders all alerts that have fired at any point in the scenario run, without temporal differentiation at the step level. The distinction between "this threshold was crossed at step 1 and remains breached" and "a new, more severe threshold was crossed at step 3" is not visually communicated.

This root cause requires investigation before a GitHub issue can be scoped: if the alert data is step-differentiated in the API response but the panel is not rendering the differentiation, it is a display issue. If the same two alerts fire at step 1 and the simulation produces no new or deepening alerts through step 5, the calibration of the MDA thresholds for the Greece programme inputs needs review.
