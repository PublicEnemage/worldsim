## Demo Flow — Persona-by-Persona Observations

### Section 1 — "The Room" (3 minutes)

The presenter opens with the challenge-response framing. IMF team, twenty analysts, proprietary
models. Finance ministry team, three economists, public data, twelve seconds to answer a question
about the source of a figure they staked their negotiating position on.

**Lucas** settles in immediately. He has been in that room — not on the ministry side, but he
has worked with ministries that were. The framing is accurate. He does not challenge it. He is
already calibrating what he will test in the application section. He notes inwardly: the question
"what is your source?" is more dangerous in practice than the question "is your number right?" —
because the former is impossible to answer confidently without the infrastructure to support it,
regardless of analytical quality. He is curious whether the tool actually delivers on the setup.

**Eleni** says nothing during Section 1. Her expression is evaluative — she is checking whether
the setup description matches what she experienced at the negotiating table in 2012 and 2015. It
does. The specific framing — "whether the challenge lands or bounces depends on how fast you can
give the source, how specific it is, whether it names the institution, the tier, and the date" —
is exactly what those sessions felt like from the ministry side. She is now paying attention.

**Andreas** registers with interest. His frame of reference is the ministerial briefing the next
morning — can he extract a political risk summary from this tool that does not require him to ask
Lucas to translate it? The setup does not address his question yet. He waits.

**Aicha** is already nodding. This is her professional reality. She does not need to be convinced
the problem exists. She has lived it. Her internal question shifts to: "Does the tool actually do
what the setup promises, or is this another demonstration of a capability that evaporates on
contact with a real negotiating situation?" Her patience for the former is limited.

### Section 2 — Live Application (8 minutes)

*The presenter has held Frame C until the final sentence of Section 1: "Today we are going to
show you what that moment looks like when the ministry team has the right tool." Only then does
Frame C appear. This resolves DEMO-100 — Aicha is oriented before the screen appears.*

**What they see:** Zone 1B WARNING badge. "Reserve Coverage (months) FIN ZMB." Current 2.908.
Floor 2.500. "Moderate confidence — cite with caveat." "16.3% above floor at step 3. 0
consecutive steps." Grounding strip: "Reserve coverage: 3.8 months · IMF WEO · 2024-Q1 · T2."

**Presenter narration (per revised walkthrough, PR #1078):** Explicitly names the dual-value
disambiguation upfront: "The Grounding strip shows 3.8 months — that is the initial value at
programme entry, sourced from IMF WEO April 2024. Zone 1B shows 2.9 months — that is the
current simulated state at step three, after two years of food price shock. They are not in
conflict. They are two points on the same trajectory." Then: "At this step, the analysis tells
us: reserve coverage is at 2.9 months, 0.4 months above the CRITICAL floor of 2.5. At the draw
rate the food price shock has set, CRITICAL is one step away. That is what the numbers on screen

**Lucas** reads Zone 1B carefully before the presenter speaks. He sees "2.908" and "2.500 floor."

**Eleni** is watching the Grounding strip. She tries the challenge-response scenario in her head:
the creditor says "we have a different number." She looks at the strip. "Reserve coverage: 3.8

**Andreas** looks at Zone 1D immediately. He sees the PSP value — approximately 0.65. He sees

**Aicha** looks at the screen and registers the WARNING badge. The "Reserve Coverage (months) FIN

#### Frame D — Political Feasibility in the Same View (Step 3, 2026)

*The presenter introduces programme survival probability (PSP).*

**Andreas** becomes fully engaged the moment the presenter says "a fifth readout: programme

the raw number alone. He understands "meaningful political execution risk" as a practitioner
phrase. He can work with it. He still does not have a reference point — what does 0.65 mean

**Lucas** hears the PSP introduction and immediately applies the same methodology interrogation

implemented?" Lucas accepts the conceptual framing. He notes the presenter says PSP is
"exploratory in M14" and follows the revised narration with a plain-language practitioner
translation: "Use it to calibrate your own risk assessment — not to argue with the creditor

**Eleni** is watching the Grounding strip state — it is now closed, as expected at step 3 in

### Section 3 — Backtesting Credibility (4 minutes)

**Lucas** is engaged throughout. He has heard backtesting claims before that did not hold up to
scrutiny. He tests each case as it is presented.

On Argentina: "Simulated contraction −10.55% against historical −10.9%. A 3.2% deviation. If
this holds on a second independent magnitude validation, I would put this in a staff working
paper as a model reference." He accepts the DIRECTION_ONLY framing on the other four cases:
"Do not claim magnitude until you have it. That is the correct discipline."

On Zambia: "You disclosed that Zambia is not yet a backtested case. I appreciate that. You are
showing a simulation output for a country the engine has not been calibrated against. That is
the honest disclosure. It also means you should not use this Zambia run as a predictive
statement in a policy brief — only as a demonstration of the engine's architecture."

On DIRECTION_ONLY: He asks about threshold calibration — specifically, the 3.0-month WARNING
and 2.5-month CRITICAL thresholds for reserve coverage. "Is the 2.5-month CRITICAL floor based
on the IMF's ARA (Assessing Reserve Adequacy) metric, or is it a standard import coverage
benchmark?" This is a specific methodology question. The presenter references the methodology
documentation and acknowledges the calibration basis should be demonstrably accessible from
within the tool.

**Eleni** listens to the Greece 2010–2012 backtesting claim. "Correct directional prediction on
fiscal consolidation outcomes. I was in the rooms where those outcomes landed. The direction is
not in dispute. The magnitude — and more importantly the distributional human cost — is what the
programmes got wrong." She waits to see whether WorldSim addresses the human cost ledger. Frame
E does not include a human development alert in this demonstration (ecological module disabled).
She files this as a question: does the tool show human cost alongside financial cost in a full
deployment?

**Andreas** notes the Ecuador recovery case. "A tool that only validates during contraction is
half a tool. Ecuador confirms the engine can distinguish stabilization from continued decline.
That matters for political advisors — the recovery signal is as important as the crisis signal."

### Section 7 — The North Star (1 minute)

The quinoa farmer closing lands.

**Aicha** says nothing. The silence is the response. The closing connects the demonstration's
technical capabilities to the human consequence the capabilities exist to serve. She has spent
fifteen years working on fiscal adjustment programmes whose human cost was inadequately modelled.
This closing does not resolve that history — but it names the discipline that would. DEMO-121
(positive finding — do not cut this) holds in the live session.

**Lucas** is less affected emotionally but registers the epistemic framing. "Build it as if he
does." For a tool that makes methodological claims, that framing implies a discipline: do not
build outputs that require specialist mediation to interpret, because the finance minister's
team has fewer specialists than the creditor side. He has heard this principle before in other
contexts. He has not seen it encoded into a product architecture before today.

**Eleni** does not speak. Her eyes are wet. She looks at the demo materials on the table and
does not pick them up.

**Andreas** notes that the closing makes the political case for the tool without requiring him
to make it for the Minister. He can show the Minister the closing. He will not have to explain
why this matters. The closing explains it.

## Capability Gaps Named by Stakeholders

The following capability gaps were named directly by stakeholders in the session — not inferred
from Q&A, but stated as explicit gaps in what would make the tool more useful at the table.

**Lucas Ferreira (P1 — IMF Country Economist):**

1. **Methodology access from within the tool.** "The methodology is published — but I need to be
   able to navigate to a specific model assumption from within the Zone 1B alert, not from a
   separate browser tab I have been told about. The trust architecture should extend to
   methodology access, not only to data source provenance."

2. **Threshold calibration basis inline.** "The 2.5-month CRITICAL floor is not self-evidently
   correct. A practitioner will ask about the calibration basis. The answer should be accessible
   from the floor value itself — a tooltip that names the ARA standard and the ZMB-specific
   calibration data."

3. **Sensitivity analysis path.** "If I wanted to run the same scenario with a food shock of
   0.25 instead of 0.35, what is the path? I need to know whether the CRITICAL floor crossing
   is shock-magnitude-dependent or structural."

**Eleni Papadimitriou (P2 — Hellenic Ministry of Finance):**

4. **Grounding strip as default-open in Reactive entry.** "The 90-second ceiling is real.
   The claim that the strip is 'still open, never closed' needs to be architecturally true —
   not presenter-controlled. If the strip closes when the user advance steps, the challenge-
   response moment depends on the analyst knowing to reopen it. That is one tap of overhead
   in a ten-second window."

5. **Inline caveat text.** "Tell me what to caveat. 'Cite with caveat' without the caveat text
   means I have to leave the screen to find out what I am supposed to say. The caveat should
   be one additional line in Zone 1B — always visible, never behind a click."

6. **Human development alongside financial trajectory.** "Reserve coverage is the financial
   story. But in a programme review, the question is also what the spending cut does to school
   enrolment and clinic hours. The four-axis architecture implies this capability exists. Show
   it in the next demonstration."

**Andreas Stefanidis (P3 — Senior Policy Advisor):**

7. **PSP self-interpreting sentence on screen.** "I can use the narrated sentence — 'meaningful
   political execution risk, not a breakdown, but an active constraint' — in a brief. I cannot
   use '0.65' without that sentence. The sentence needs to be on the screen, not in the
   presenter's notes. Until it is, I need Lucas in the room."

8. **Historical calibration anchor for PSP.** "I work in historical pattern recognition. Every
   number I brief to the Minister has a precedent. 'PSP 0.65' does not have one yet. The first
   question my Minister will ask is: 'Has any programme with a PSP at this level succeeded? Has
   one failed?' I need to be able to answer that."

9. **PSP trajectory across steps.** "If PSP is declining alongside the reserve arc, the
   compounding story is visible. If PSP is stable while reserves deteriorate, that is a different
   political brief. Show me PSP at each step, not only at step 3."

**Aicha Mbaye (P5 — Finance Minister, Senegal):**

10. **Mode 3 counter-proposal branch.** "The most important moment in this demonstration is when
    you describe what M15 will add: the counter-proposal branch. Running the scenario with
    alternative conditionality structures and seeing the reserve arc for each branch — that is
    the capability that changes what I can say at the table. Everything else is preparation.
    That is the capability."

11. **PSP plain-language translation on screen.** "The sentence the presenter narrated — 'the
    conditionality terms carry meaningful political execution risk' — is a sentence I can use.
    The number 0.65 I cannot use without the sentence. I will wait for M15."
