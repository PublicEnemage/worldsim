## 2. Historical Context

Pakistan's 2022–23 IMF engagement is the 23rd IMF programme in Pakistan's post-independence history — a statistic that captures the country's chronic balance-of-payments fragility. The 2022 episode is analytically distinct from prior programmes because it involves two simultaneous stress tests: the intrinsic conditionality challenge (removing fuel subsidies under political constraint) and an exogenous catastrophe (the August 2022 floods, which submerged one-third of the country and caused USD 30bn in damage).

**Crisis context (2022 H1 initial conditions):**
Pakistan entered 2022 H1 with forex reserves at approximately USD 11.3bn (~1.9 months import cover), GDP growth at 5.97% (strong, but fuelled partly by subsidised energy that was fiscally unsustainable), and an EMBI spread of ~450 bps (reflecting market distress ahead of programme entry). The IMF's Extended Fund Facility (EFF) was already in a suspension cycle from 2019; the 2022 crisis was precipitated by fuel subsidy reinstatement under PM Khan in early 2022, followed by his removal in April 2022.

**Historical arc (4 biannual steps, 2022 H1 → 2023 H2):**
- **Step 1 (2022 H1):** IMF conditionality engagement — fuel subsidy removal (full, front-loaded). Political feasibility was approximately 0.6: the Khan government had partially reversed prior subsidy commitments, and the incoming Shehbaz government faced enormous political cost in implementing IMF terms.
- **Step 2 (2022 H2):** Energy pricing structural reform + August 2022 floods (catastrophic exogenous shock — ~USD 30bn damage, 1,700+ deaths, 33 million affected). IMF approved a USD 1.1bn tranche release in August 2022 despite programme stress.
- **Step 3 (2023 H1):** SBP tightening — policy rate raised to ~21% (highest in decades); inflation peaked at ~38% YoY. The Stand-By Arrangement (SBA, USD 3bn, 9 months) agreed July 2023 replaced the EFF.
- **Step 4 (2023 H2):** SBA stabilisation — rate hold, fiscal primary surplus targets. Pakistan avoided sovereign default, unlike Sri Lanka.

**Counter-factual hypothesis:** The front-loaded conditionality in Step 1 (full fuel subsidy removal at implementation_capacity=0.6) created severe short-term political and economic stress. What if the removal had been phased — 25% per step over all four steps with higher implementation feasibility (capacity=0.85 per step)? Does phased delivery produce better human development outcomes despite taking four steps to complete the same total adjustment?

---

## 3. Question Posed

**Type A:** Does the engine find declining financial and human development composites at Step 1 (front-loaded fuel subsidy removal under IMF conditionality), consistent with Pakistan's documented economic hardship in 2022 H1–H2?

**Type B:** Does the engine find that phased conditionality (25% per step, implementation_capacity=0.85) produces better human development outcomes than front-loaded conditionality at Steps 2–3, consistent with political economy theory that phased adjustment reduces peak social cost?

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **Steps 3–4 identical (flat response to monetary tightening):** The SBP policy rate increase (+3pp at Step 3) produces no fin_composite or hd_composite change. Pakistan's 2023 monetary tightening to ~21% was one of the highest real policy rates in emerging markets at the time — the engine does not detect this as a detectable composite signal. The flat Steps 3–4 pattern is now observed across four AEP entries (GHA, ZMB, PAK; with LKA also showing early-step flatness) and represents a systematic engine limitation for multi-step adjustment propagation.

- [x] **PSP null throughout:** The fixture was explicitly designed to stress-test the PoliticalEconomyModule's `programme_survival_probability` — the primary headline design objective from Issue #1550. PSP is null at all four steps. Neither front-loaded conditionality nor phased conditionality generates PSP values. This is a significant gap: the most interesting analytical claim from this fixture (whether implementation_capacity=0.6 produces programme survival stress) cannot be demonstrated.

- [x] **Failure modes not detected:** Get-There-Itis (commitment escalation through 22 prior programme exits without structural change) and Hypoxia (political interference in SBP and MoF decisions) are both documented historically and explicitly named in the fixture design. Neither fires at any step. Consistent with Issue #1797.

- [x] **Remittance channel absent:** PAK remittances are approximately 8% GDP — one of the highest in the SOUTH-SE-ASIAN family. This channel is absent from CM-C calibration. For Pakistan specifically, remittances are a structural stabilising buffer: the external balance is partially maintained by diaspora transfers even during balance-of-payments stress. The absence of this channel means the engine understates the external balance stabilisation at Steps 3–4 and overstates the expected fin_composite decline.

- [x] **August 2022 floods not explicitly modelled:** The Step 2 fixture uses a `regulatory_change` (energy pricing reform) but does not explicitly model the August 2022 flood shock (USD 30bn damage, ~5% GDP). The flood's impact on agriculture, infrastructure, and Q1 poverty headcount is absent from the harness output. Step 2's fin and hd decline may partially reflect the regulatory change but not the catastrophic flood.

- [x] **Type B CF Step 1 zero differential:** The CF Step 1 spending_change (-0.009) is smaller than the baseline effective Step 1 shock (-0.021 after implementation capacity scaling). Despite this difference, the Step 1 per_step_diff is 0.0000. The smaller Step 1 adjustment has not produced any detectable composite differentiation yet at Step 1 — differentiation only appears from Step 2. This suggests the engine's propagation delay is at least one step for fiscal adjustment shocks.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

**Type A:** The engine finds modest but directionally correct decline for Pakistan's front-loaded conditionality path: both composites fall at Step 2 (after the fuel subsidy removal propagates) and then hold flat. This is a partial success — the engine detects that the conditionality produces hardship in the period following implementation. The magnitude is small (hd: 0.8571 → 0.8259, -3.6%) and the plateau at Steps 3–4 means the engine misses the continued economic hardship documented in Pakistan's 2023 tightening cycle. But the direction for Steps 1–2 is correct.

Critically, PAK's Type A result (modest decline, then stable) provides a useful contrast with LKA AEP-010 (counterintuitive improvement): the engine correctly distinguishes between a country that managed its IMF programme without default (PAK: partial decline, stabilisation) and a country that defaulted (LKA: incorrectly shows improvement). The relative ordering — LKA initial fin slightly higher than PAK (0.5927 vs 0.5782), reflecting LKA's 2019 pre-crisis advantage — is reasonable.

**Type B:** The engine finds `COUNTER_FACTUAL_BETTER` for phased conditionality — consistent with the political-economy hypothesis that smaller per-step adjustments at higher implementation feasibility produce better human development outcomes during the adjustment period. The peak differential at Step 2 (+0.0232 hd) is modest but directionally confirmed. The narrowing differential toward Step 4 is consistent with both paths converging to the same long-run fiscal position.

For a Pakistani finance ministry analyst, this entry demonstrates: (1) the engine recognises that front-loaded conditionality produces short-term hardship; (2) the engine finds that phased delivery reduces peak social cost. These are directional claims only — the specific magnitude of the hd differential cannot be asserted under T3 calibration.
