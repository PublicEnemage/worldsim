## 2. Historical Context

Ghana's 2022–23 crisis is one of the canonical CB Cloud / Coffin Corner cases in the WorldSim evidence base. Ghana lost Eurobond market access in 2022 before an IMF programme was secured — the defining Coffin Corner geometry: the exit corridor (IMF programme) had not yet opened before the fuel ran out (market access lost). The Domestic Debt Exchange Programme (DDEP) announced in December 2022 imposed mandatory haircuts on domestic bondholders — including pension funds, retail investors, and households — while external commercial creditors were protected from immediate restructuring. This is the CB Cloud failure mode: distributional asymmetry in a creditor-absorbing restructuring, where the most financially exposed domestic actors bear the largest share of the adjustment cost.

Ghana's IMF Extended Credit Facility (ECF) programme was approved in May 2023, providing USD 3bn over three years with fiscal consolidation conditionality requiring approximately 3% of GDP in adjustment. The DDEP was the domestic-debt leg of a broader debt restructuring. Reserve coverage improved through the programme period as IMF disbursements restored Central Bank of Ghana reserve buffers. But Q1 poverty headcount continued rising through 2023: the consolidation-induced contraction, combined with the DDEP's direct haircut impact on domestic savings, hit lower-income households disproportionately.

The scenario is structurally interesting because it requires the engine to reproduce simultaneous directional signals: financial stabilisation (reserve recovery) alongside continued human development deterioration — the CB Cloud characteristic. This entry documents whether the engine produces that directional split.

## 3. Question Posed

**Question:** Does the engine find declining human development composites alongside declining (then stabilising) financial composites across Ghana's 2022–23 IMF programme period — consistent with the CB Cloud: financial stabilisation at the cost of distributional harm to Q1 poverty-cohort households?

**Scenario type rationale:** Type A historical replay. The scheduled inputs replicate the Eurobond debt moratorium (Step 1), DDEP haircut and IMF ECF programme acceptance (Step 2), and ECF fiscal consolidation continuation (Steps 3–4). Four biannual steps (2022 H1 through 2023 H2).

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **Steps 1–2 identical values:** The engine produces no differentiation between the debt moratorium and the IMF programme / DDEP haircut step. Both composite values are identical at Steps 1 and 2. The moratorium's immediate financial impact is not captured as a distinct composite signal.
- [x] **CB Cloud distributional asymmetry not reproduced:** The engine does not produce the simultaneous fin-stabilisation + hd-deterioration pattern that characterises Ghana's documented CB Cloud. Both composites decline together from Step 3. This is the most significant fidelity gap in this entry relative to the fixture's design intention. This is a composite aggregation issue analogous to Issue #1796: the IMF programme is modelled and reserve disbursements likely affect the reserve coverage channel at the engine level, but the fin_composite aggregates reserve recovery and fiscal consolidation contractionary effects into a single net signal — the contractionary fiscal effect dominates, masking the reserve recovery. The divergence between what is happening inside two components of the same composite (reserve coverage improving; fiscal position worsening) is not surfaceable through the current composite architecture. This is distinct from the signal being completely absent: the programme inputs are active, but the composite aggregation cannot reproduce the within-composite divergence that defines the CB Cloud pattern.
- [x] **Coffin Corner and CB Cloud failure modes not detected:** Ghana 2022 is a canonical case for both Coffin Corner (market access loss before programme secured) and CB Cloud (domestic small bondholder haircut). Neither failure mode fires. Consistent with Issue #1797.
- [x] **Reserve recovery not visible in fin_composite:** The IMF disbursements improved Bank of Ghana reserve coverage through the programme period. This financial stabilisation signal does not appear in fin_composite — the engine does not differentiate between the distributional components of the financial composite.
- [x] **DDEP haircut transmission:** The Domestic Debt Exchange Programme imposed direct losses on domestic retail and institutional bondholders. The engine models this as a fiscal debt_issuance input but does not capture the direct wealth destruction for domestic savings holders or the resulting consumption constraint on Q1 poverty cohort households.
- [x] **Biannual step resolution:** The 6-month step interval means each step covers a significant period. The differentiation between H1 and H2 2022 events (moratorium at Step 1, IMF programme at Step 2) is not producing distinct composite signals.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

The engine finds declining financial and human development composites for Ghana's 2022–23 IMF programme period, directionally consistent with the documented ECF adjustment cost. The sharpest decline begins at Step 3 (2023 H1), after the IMF programme terms begin propagating through the fiscal channel. However, the engine fails to reproduce two key features of Ghana's crisis: (1) the Steps 1–2 plateau shows no differentiation between the debt moratorium and the DDEP haircut event; and (2) the CB Cloud distributional signal — financial stabilisation alongside continued Q1 poverty deterioration — is absent. Both composites decline together rather than showing the divergent pattern documented historically.

For an analyst evaluating Ghana's 2022–23 policy response, the engine provides the correct directional trajectory (adjustment costs propagate) but does not provide the distributional insight (who bore those costs). The CB Cloud feature of this crisis — the most analytically important aspect of the Ghana case for WorldSim's mission — is not currently within the engine's detection capability.
