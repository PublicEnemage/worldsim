## 2. Historical Context

Ecuador's 1999–2000 crisis combined multiple structural failures within approximately 18 months: a banking system collapse, hyperinflation, sovereign default on Brady bonds, and the replacement of the national currency with the US dollar — all occurring while the country's major export revenue source (oil) faced suppressed prices and El Niño had recently damaged agricultural production.

**1999 — The crisis year:**
Ecuador entered 1999 with a fragile banking system carrying accumulated non-performing loans from a 1990s credit boom. In March 1999, President Mahuad froze bank deposits (the "salvazo") in an attempt to arrest bank runs — a capital control measure equivalent to USD 3bn in frozen deposits. A one-week banking holiday was declared. Despite these measures, 16 banks failed, including Filanbanco (the largest), requiring government intervention at a total cost of approximately 20–25% of GDP. The sucre collapsed from ~6,800 per USD in early 1999 to ~25,000 per USD by year-end — approximately 70% depreciation. Ecuador's annual inflation rate reached ~96% by year-end. In September 1999, Ecuador defaulted on its Brady bonds — the first Brady bond default in history — restructuring approximately USD 6.7bn in external debt.

**2000 — Dollarisation and stabilisation:**
President Mahuad announced dollarisation on January 9, 2000, fixing the exchange rate at 25,000 sucres per USD. The decision was politically explosive: within days (January 21), a military-backed popular uprising ousted Mahuad. Vice President Gustavo Noboa assumed the presidency and completed the dollarisation despite the political transition. Dollarisation terminated hyperinflation, restored monetary credibility, and attracted an IMF Stand-By Agreement (April 2000). Oil price recovery (Brent rose from ~USD 10/bbl in 1998 to ~USD 30/bbl in 2000) also contributed significantly. Ecuador's GDP grew +2.8% in 2000 — a recovery from the −6.3% outturn in 1999.

This two-step scenario is the shortest in the G2 evidence base: just two annual steps covering the crisis (1999) and the first year of dollarised recovery (2000).

## 3. Question Posed

**Question:** Does the engine find declining financial composites at Step 1 (1999 banking collapse and Brady default), consistent with the documented crisis depth, and does Step 2 show stabilisation or partial recovery consistent with the documented dollarisation stabilisation effect?

**Scenario type rationale:** Type A historical replay. The scenario covers a two-step arc: Step 1 (1999) applies the banking deposit freeze and bank holiday; Step 2 (2000) applies the dollarisation as an INSTITUTIONAL_REFORM. The Step 2 recovery expectation is explicitly limited by the fixture's documented blindspot: dollarisation stabilisation effects (monetary credibility restoration, oil price recovery) are not modelled by the engine's current scope — only the structural event is recorded.

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **Dollarisation mechanism not modelled:** The engine's most significant blindspot for this entry. Ecuador's 2000 recovery was driven by dollarisation's termination of hyperinflation and restoration of monetary credibility — effects that require a structural module not currently implemented. The INSTITUTIONAL_REFORM input records the event; the engine does not respond to it with a GDP-positive signal. The Step 2 improvement is shock attenuation, not modelled dollarisation benefit.
- [x] **Oil price recovery not captured:** Ecuador's 2000 GDP growth was substantially driven by oil price recovery (USD 10 → USD 30/bbl). No commodity price shock mechanism exists in the engine. This is an additional structural blindspot for oil-dependent economies at Step 2.
- [x] **Two-step window limits analytical depth:** With only two steps, this entry is the shallowest in the G2 evidence base. A single step cannot capture medium-term trajectory dynamics. The banking collapse, currency collapse, and dollarisation all occur within approximately 14 months — the annual timestep resolution compresses the full crisis arc into one step.
- [x] **Banking collapse distributional dynamics not captured:** The deposit freeze trapped approximately USD 3bn in household and business savings. The distributional impact — freeze hurting Q1 households who relied on banks for savings — is not captured in the human development composite. The engine's hd_composite improvement at Step 2 does not capture the household financial losses from frozen deposits.
- [x] **Brady bond default not distinguished from sovereign default:** Ecuador's Brady bond default (September 1999) is structurally different from a full sovereign default (as in Argentina 2002) — it was a strategic partial default negotiated under IMF agreement, not a unilateral stoppage. The engine may treat it as equivalent, which would understate Ecuador's post-1999 creditworthiness recovery.
- [x] **Failure modes not detected:** Ecuador 1999 is a canonical multi-failure-mode case — banking collapse, hyperinflation, currency flight, external default — none detected. Consistent with Issue #1797.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

The engine finds Ecuador's 1999 crisis reflected in a low Step 1 fin_composite (0.2458), consistent with the documented banking collapse, deposit freeze, and Brady bond default. Step 2 shows marginal improvement (+5.7% fin, +0.7% hd) — the engine finds no further deterioration at dollarisation, consistent with the fixture's minimum fidelity benchmark.

This is the most limited entry in the G2 evidence base in terms of what can be concluded: two steps, a documented structural blindspot (dollarisation mechanism), and a second structural blindspot (oil price channel) mean the engine is documenting one step of crisis and one step of "not worse." The analytical value of this entry is primarily as a LATAM-EM family calibration data point showing crisis depth rather than as a recovery arc analysis.

The CBG Cloud and Coffin Corner features of Ecuador's 1999 experience — deposit freeze asymmetry, market access loss before IMF programme secured — are not detected by the engine. For an Ecuadorian finance ministry analyst, this entry demonstrates the engine knows the country was in severe distress in 1999 and did not worsen in 2000, but cannot currently model why the dollarisation worked or attribute the recovery to specific channels.
