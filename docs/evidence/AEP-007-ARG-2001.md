## 2. Historical Context

Argentina's 2001–2003 crisis is among the most intensively studied sovereign defaults in economic history. The convertibility system — a currency board pegging the Argentine peso 1:1 to the US dollar, established in 1991 under Economy Minister Cavallo — collapsed under the weight of a 1999 recession triggered by Brazil's devaluation, chronic current account deficits, and an external debt burden that had become structurally unsustainable at the fixed exchange rate.

The crisis sequence modelled here runs across three steps:

**Step 1 (2001):** The IMF Blindaje — an augmented USD 39.7bn extended credit facility announced December 2000 and reinforced in June 2001 — was supposed to restore market confidence. Simultaneously, Economy Minister Cavallo imposed the Zero Deficit Plan (Plan Déficit Cero) in July 2001: a mandatory directive that federal expenditures could not exceed revenues in any given month, requiring an immediate pro-cyclical spending cut of approximately 6.5% of GDP. The combined effect — large external programme, extreme pro-cyclical adjustment — failed to restore confidence. Deposit withdrawals accelerated through late 2001.

**Step 2 (2002):** The corralito (December 1, 2001) — a bank deposit freeze limiting withdrawals — provoked riots and the resignation of President De la Rúa (December 20). By the end of December, Argentina had declared the world's largest sovereign default to that date: USD 81.8 billion in external debt. Pesification in January 2002 formally ended the convertibility era; the peso depreciated ~70% on liberalisation.

**Step 3 (2003):** Néstor Kirchner was inaugurated in May 2003 (after Eduardo Duhalde's interim period stabilised the immediate post-default emergency). Kirchner maintained the emergency employment program PJJHD, normalised government services after the 2002 emergency contraction, and expanded social transfers. GDP grew 8.8% in 2003 — a V-shaped recovery anchored by peso undervaluation, export tax revenues (especially from soybean boom), and suppressed utility tariffs.

**Counter-factual hypothesis (Type B):** The 1999 managed peg exit. Had Argentina abandoned the convertibility peg in late 1999 — earlier, before the crisis deepened into a full corralito and sovereign default — what trajectory would have followed? The counter-factual draws on Mussa (2002) and Calvo (2002) hypotheses: that an earlier, managed devaluation, while deeply painful, would have avoided the worst of the 2001–2002 social emergency. The counter-factual starts from 1999 initial conditions (GDP growth -3.4%, unemployment 12.9%) — a worse starting point than the Type A baseline's 2000 conditions — but applies a different shock sequence: no IMF Blindaje, no corralito, no full default.

## 3. Question Posed

**Type A:** Does the engine correctly reproduce the direction of Argentina's financial collapse (sovereign default → fin_composite collapse at Step 2) and the onset of human development deterioration (hd_composite decline Steps 1–3) across the 2001–2003 arc?

**Type B:** Does the engine find that the 1999 managed peg exit counter-factual produces better human development outcomes than the historical crisis arc (Type A baseline), consistent with the Mussa/Calvo hypothesis that an earlier managed exit would have avoided the worst of the social emergency?

**Type A narrative:**

The most striking feature of the Type A output is the fin_composite collapse to exactly 0.0000 at Step 2, where the sovereign default is declared. The CI band also collapses to [0.0000, 0.0000] — there is no uncertainty range at this step, suggesting the default declaration input drives the fin_composite to a hard floor in the engine. This is directionally correct: a sovereign default is the most severe financial event modelled, and the fin_composite signal at Step 2 reflects this appropriately.

The hd_composite declines from 0.5464 (Step 1) to 0.3723 (Step 2) and holds flat at Step 3. The Step 3 value shows no recovery signal despite the Kirchner +3% GDP spending_change — the hd_composite does not improve at Step 3. This is a noteworthy fidelity gap: Argentina's hd recovery was measurable by 2003 (GDP +8.8%, unemployment beginning to fall), and the engine does not capture this at the composite level.

PSP is null across all steps — the scenario involves no active IMF programme conditionality at Step 3 (the Blindaje expired at Step 2, per `expected_duration: 2`).

**Type B narrative:**

The engine finds `COUNTER_FACTUAL_BETTER` with first significant divergence at Step 1. The per_step_diff grows monotonically: +0.0643 at Step 1, +0.1670 at Step 2, +0.2027 at Step 3. The largest differential is at Step 3 — the Kirchner recovery step in the historical baseline, where the CF shows hd_composite of 0.5750 vs the baseline's 0.3723.

The CF fin_composite is lower than the baseline at Step 1 (0.4125 vs 0.5750) — the 1999 managed exit itself imposes financial costs. But the CF fin_composite recovers partially at Step 3 (0.2875 vs 0.0000 in the baseline) — the CF avoids the zero-floor default event.

The per_step_diff is entirely driven by hd_composite: the baseline's hd collapse at Step 2 (from 0.5464 to 0.3723) produces the differential that the CF avoids. The direction claim is that avoiding the corralito and full default produces better human development outcomes, consistent with the Mussa/Calvo hypothesis.

**Important note on fin_composite pattern:** The Type B harness records show the CF fin_composite is higher than baseline at Steps 2–3 despite the CF starting from a weaker financial position (1999 initial state). This reflects that the CF avoids the default declaration's catastrophic fin_composite impact. The fin_composite path insensitivity (#1796) noted in G1 entries may also affect this comparison: the CF fin values at 0.1625/0.2875 may not be capturing the full distributional difference between a managed devaluation and a chaotic default.

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **LATAM-EM T3 across all indicators:** No country-specific multiplier calibration for Argentina. All composite magnitudes are INFERRED_STATISTICAL. The DIRECTION_ONLY ceiling applies to both Type A and Type B components.
- [x] **Type A Step 3 recovery not detected:** The Kirchner +3% GDP spending_change at Step 3 produces no hd_composite improvement. The historical V-shaped recovery (GDP +8.8% in 2003) is directionally inconsistent with the engine's output at Step 3. This is a fidelity gap for the engine's ability to model rapid recovery from fiscal normalisation.
- [x] **Type A CI collapse at Steps 2–3:** Both fin_composite and CI band collapse to [0.0000, 0.0000] at Steps 2–3. The engine produces a hard-floor outcome at the default step with no uncertainty envelope. This may reflect appropriate parameterisation of a sovereign default event, or may indicate a CI calculation gap when the base value is at the floor. Further investigation warranted.
- [x] **Type B counter-factual inputs are INFERRED_STRUCTURAL (T3):** The 1999 managed peg exit was not negotiated or executed. The initial state (GDP -3.4%, unemployment 12.9%) is documented 1999 data, but the control inputs for the managed exit path are structural inferences from Mussa (2002) / Calvo (2002). Direction verdict is advisory.
- [x] **Type B CF framing note:** The CF initial state (1999) is 2 years earlier than the baseline initial state (2001). The three-step comparison is not fully comparable in calendar-year terms — the CF avoids 2000–2001 deterioration that the baseline assumes as the starting condition. The per_step_diff reflects both the earlier exit and the crisis avoidance.
- [x] **Failure modes not detected:** Argentina 2001 is a canonical rapid-onset financial crisis with a multi-failure-mode character (capital flight → deposit freeze → corralito → default). No failure modes detected at any step. Consistent with Issue #1797 and the absence of endogenous failure mode detection for LATAM-EM rapid-onset crises.
- [x] **fin_composite path insensitivity (#1796):** The Type B CF fin values (0.1625 at Step 2; 0.2875 at Step 3) may not fully differentiate the economic consequences of a managed devaluation from a chaotic default. The hd_composite differential is the reliable directional signal; fin_composite differences are advisory.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

**Type A:** The engine correctly identifies Argentina's 2001–2002 crisis arc as a catastrophic financial event: fin_composite collapses to zero at the sovereign default step, and hd_composite declines sharply. The engine does not reproduce the Kirchner recovery signal at Step 3 — the 8.8% GDP growth in 2003 is not visible in hd_composite. This is the most significant Type A fidelity gap for this entry.

**Type B:** The engine finds `COUNTER_FACTUAL_BETTER` with a growing differential across all three steps, driven entirely by the CF's avoidance of the fin_composite zero-floor and the associated hd_composite collapse. The direction claim — that the 1999 managed exit produces better human development outcomes than the historical crisis arc — is consistent with the Mussa/Calvo hypothesis, but the specific magnitude (+0.2027 at Step 3) is advisory under LATAM-EM T3 calibration.

For a finance ministry analyst using this entry: the engine correctly signals that sovereign default is catastrophic at the composite level, and that an earlier managed exit (if achievable) would have avoided the worst of the human development deterioration. But it does not capture the recovery velocity (Step 3 gap) or the specific distributional dynamics (which cohorts bore the devaluation vs default costs).
