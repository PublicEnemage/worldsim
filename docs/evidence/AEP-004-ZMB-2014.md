## 2. Historical Context

Zambia entered 2014 at a moment of apparent stability. Copper prices had supported steady GDP growth through the commodity supercycle, and Zambia had issued its first Eurobond in September 2012 (USD 750M at 5.375%), signalling market confidence in sovereign creditworthiness. But the structural vulnerabilities were already in place: copper represented over 70% of Zambia's export earnings, fiscal revenue was closely tied to mining royalties and corporate taxes on copper production, and the Kwacha had appreciated against the dollar in ways that penalised non-mining exporters.

The copper price crash of 2014–2016 — LME copper fell from ~USD 7,000/t in early 2014 to below USD 4,500/t by early 2016, approximately a 37% decline — exposed these vulnerabilities with severity. The Kwacha depreciated ~45% against the dollar in 2015 alone, dramatically raising the domestic-currency cost of servicing the Eurobond. The fiscal deficit widened to over 7% of GDP by 2016 as revenue collapsed and social spending commitments were not adjusted. Zambia issued a second Eurobond (USD 1bn, 9.375%, April 2014) and a third (USD 1.25bn, January 2015) at progressively higher yields, deepening the external debt burden at the worst possible moment in the commodity cycle.

By 2019 — the terminal year of this scenario — Zambia was on an established pre-default trajectory. Secondary market spreads on its Eurobonds had widened to over 1,500 basis points, equivalent to a deep distressed designation. An IMF Staff Monitored Program (without financing) had failed to achieve fiscal consolidation targets. Zambia would formally default on its external commercial debt in November 2020. The six-year arc from near-peak conditions (2014) to default threshold (2019) is the scenario this entry documents.

## 3. Question Posed

**Question:** Does the engine consistently find declining financial and human development composites across Zambia's 2014–2019 copper-crash-to-default arc, consistent with the documented historical trajectory of fiscal deterioration, currency depreciation, and growing debt distress?

**Scenario type rationale:** This is a Type A historical replay. The scheduled inputs replicate the documented policy sequence (Eurobond issuances, IMF Staff Monitored Programme, copper price shock transmitted through the fiscal channel). The harness output is evaluated for directional consistency with the known historical arc, not point-estimate accuracy.

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **SSA-LIC fiscal multiplier is T3 (Fosu 2011):** The Fosu 2011 estimate is derived from a cross-sectional SSA sample; Zambia-specific multiplier calibration is not available. All fiscal-channel magnitude claims are INFERRED_STATISTICAL. The DIRECTION_ONLY ceiling reflects this — the engine's direction claim is credible, but the specific composite values are advisory.
- [x] **Commodity price shock not directly modelled:** The copper price crash is the primary driver of Zambia's 2014–2019 deterioration. The engine models this through fiscal and reserve channel transmission (reduced revenue, currency depreciation, reserve drawdown), not through a direct commodity price shock mechanism. The channel is correct directionally; the magnitude of transmission is approximated.
- [x] **Eurobond issuance dynamics not modelled:** Zambia issued three Eurobonds across the scenario period ($750M, $1bn, $1.25bn), increasing external debt at rising yields. The engine captures the general external debt pressure but does not model bond issuance, secondary market spread dynamics, or yield escalation. The path to distress is compressed into fiscal-channel flows.
- [x] **Failure mode non-detection:** The approach to the 2020 default is not detected as a failure mode. Zambia's trajectory — sustained fiscal deficit above threshold, rising external debt service, declining reserve coverage — is a structurally coherent Backside_of_Power_Curve candidate, but the engine does not fire this mode. Consistent with Issue #1797.
- [x] **CI width at Step 6:** The Step 6 CI band spans 0.44 units ([0.0740, 0.5178]), nearly the full range of the fin_composite scale. At this uncertainty level, the confidence interval provides no useful constraint on the magnitude estimate. The directional trajectory is preserved but the specific Step 6 value (0.2959) is unreliable as a magnitude claim.
- [x] **Focal cohort poverty headcount:** The ZMB fixture includes a monitored focal cohort (Q1 poverty headcount). These are not reported in the AEP harness summary for this entry — the per_step_records contain null for `cohort_poverty_headcount`. Future authorship may populate this if the harness is run with cohort reporting enabled.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

The engine finds that Zambia's copper-crash-to-default arc produces consistent financial and human development deterioration across the 2014–2019 window, with the sharpest declines concentrated in 2016 (Step 3, fiscal deficit peak) and 2019 (Step 6, pre-default trajectory). The fin_composite drops by approximately half over the six-year window; hd_composite falls by approximately 28%. Both trends are directionally consistent with Zambia's documented trajectory: rising external debt distress, declining reserve coverage, growing poverty headcount, and deteriorating fiscal position.

This is a directional claim only. The SSA-LIC T3 fiscal multiplier means the specific composite magnitudes are advisory; the wide CI band at Step 6 (±0.22) illustrates the calibration uncertainty. A Zambian finance ministry analyst using this entry can rely on the engine to identify the *direction* of deterioration and the *relative severity* between steps — the Step 6 deterioration is meaningfully worse than Step 2 across both dimensions — but should not treat the specific numeric values as calibrated estimates.

The engine's failure to detect any failure mode despite Zambia's documented approach to the 2020 default threshold is a known limitation that reduces the actionability of this entry for early warning purposes.
