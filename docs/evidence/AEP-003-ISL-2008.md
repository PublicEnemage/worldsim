## 2. Historical Context

In October 2008, Iceland's three major banks — Kaupthing, Landsbanki, and Glitnir — collapsed simultaneously, with combined liabilities approximately 10 times Iceland's GDP. This was the largest banking collapse relative to national economic size in modern history. Iceland faced a binary choice: absorb the banking sector liabilities onto the sovereign balance sheet (the orthodox IMF prescription), or let the banks fail and protect domestic depositors through nationalisation while imposing capital controls to halt capital flight.

Iceland chose the heterodox path. Capital controls were imposed on 28 October 2008, preventing foreign creditors from liquidating ISK assets. The domestic operations of the failed banks were carved out into new state-owned institutions, protecting household and business deposits while foreign creditors — including UK and Dutch retail depositors in Icesave accounts — absorbed losses. Household debt was restructured via krona-indexed mortgage writedowns. Fiscal adjustment was comparatively modest (-2% GDP at Step 1 vs the -8% the orthodox prescription would have required).

The outcome was exceptional by the standards of financial crisis recovery. Iceland returned to positive GDP growth by 2011 — faster than any of the Troika programme countries (Ireland, Greece, Portugal). Unemployment peaked lower and declined faster. The heterodox choice imposed severe costs on foreign creditors and Iceland's international reputation, but preserved domestic economic capacity in ways the orthodox alternative arguably would not have.

The orthodox counter-factual models what the IMF-orthodox prescription would have produced: sovereign debt assumption for banking liabilities, deep austerity, and an open capital account. This path was not taken. Its inputs are INFERRED_STRUCTURAL (Tier 3) — designed to represent the structural equivalent of standard IMF conditionality circa 2008, not a negotiated programme.

## 3. Question Posed

**Question:** Does the engine find a meaningfully better human development trajectory under Iceland's actual heterodox path (capital controls + nationalisation + household debt relief) than under the orthodox counter-factual (deep austerity + open capital account + sovereign bailout)?

**Scenario type rationale:** This is a Type B counter-factual — the orthodox path was not historically executed. The **baseline** in this entry is the heterodox path (what actually happened); the **counter-factual** is the orthodox path (what did not happen). A `BASELINE_BETTER` verdict means the engine finds the heterodox baseline outperforms the orthodox counter-factual on `hd_composite` — consistent with the historical recovery record.

## 7. Known Limitations

- [x] **Orthodox counter-factual inputs are INFERRED_STRUCTURAL (Tier 3):** The IMF-orthodox prescription for Iceland 2008 was not negotiated or executed. The -8% GDP Step 1 austerity figure is calibrated to represent standard IMF conditionality of the period (consistent with Ireland 2010, Portugal 2011 programmes) but has no Iceland-specific anchor. The DIRECTION_ONLY verdict reflects this limitation.
- [x] **Sovereign debt assumption not directly modelled:** The orthodox path would have Iceland absorbing ~80% GDP of banking liabilities onto the sovereign balance sheet. This structural feature is approximated through fiscal deterioration dynamics, not explicitly modelled as a balance sheet transaction. The full fiscal sustainability impact of sovereign bank bailout is not captured.
- [x] **fin_composite does not differentiate the two paths:** Both branches show 0.1458 from Step 2. The capital controls channel produces hd_composite differentiation but not fin_composite differentiation. This is a gap in how the engine aggregates capital controls effects into the financial composite.
- [x] **hd_composite unavailable at Step 1:** The trajectory endpoint does not populate `hd_composite` for the first step. The comparison window is Steps 2–4 (3 of 4 steps).
- [x] **Failure modes not detected:** Iceland's 2008 collapse is a canonical rapid-onset crisis — rapid external debt collapse, currency flight, bank run. None of the six aviation failure modes fired in the counter-factual run. The engine does not detect the structural preconditions for these failure modes in the orthodox path. This is a known engine limitation for rapid-onset banking crises.
- [x] **Iceland Q1 household debt overhang not captured:** The heterodox recovery was partly delayed by dollarised mortgage debt overhang on household balance sheets. ADR-020 Channel C targets Q1 informal PHC but does not model the household debt stock channel specifically. The recovery speed in the baseline may be overstated relative to historical outturn.
- [x] **Bilateral creditor composition not modelled:** The UK/Netherlands Icesave dispute (foreign retail depositor claims) is a significant feature of Iceland's orthodox-path costs. Bilateral creditor composition is not modelled — the counter-factual does not capture the diplomatic and legal friction this dispute would have imposed.
- [x] **ADR-020 channel magnitudes not empirically validated for Iceland:** The capital controls channels in ADR-020 are calibrated from the general EURO-AREA family. Iceland-specific validation against the 2008–2011 recovery data has not been performed. The MAGNITUDE tier is not available for this entry.

## 8. Plain-Language Synthesis

The engine finds that Iceland's heterodox policy response — capital controls, bank nationalisation, and household debt relief — produces meaningfully better human development outcomes than the orthodox alternative (deep austerity, open capital account, sovereign bank bailout) from 2009 onward. The hd_composite gap grows from −0.14 in 2009 to −0.33 by 2011, with the heterodox path consistently outperforming. This is a directional claim only: the orthodox inputs are structural inferences, not a historically executed programme, and the ADR-020 capital controls channel magnitudes have not been validated against Iceland-specific data. The finding is consistent with the documented historical record — Iceland recovered faster than comparable Troika-programme countries — but this entry cannot assert that the magnitude of the engine's differential matches Iceland's actual recovery advantage.
